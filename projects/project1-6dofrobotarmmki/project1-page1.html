6-DoF Robot Arm

Part 1 - Challenge By Choice

Austin Wells - February 28, 2022

During the Fall 2021 semester, I was enrolled in "Mechatronics Design", an introductory course in mechatronics engineering. As part of this course, students were given the rather nebulous task of applying what they had learned in building mechatronics systems for a final group project. Assessment would be based on three categories: interest level, presentation quality, and technical sophistication.

Not wanting to be outdone, one of my friends with similar hobbyist experience and I teamed up and set out with the goal of creating the most advanced project of all the students in the class. Mooted ideas included a kinetic sand art table, autonomous egg launcher, and more but in the end, we settled on building a more traditional 6 degree-of-freedom robot arm as used in many industrial applications. The catch: we had only six weeks to design, build, and test the system.

To start, we formulated a list of performance specifications and functionalities, ordered by priority. Some of the more advanced or ambitious elements of our list were identified as 'stretch' goals while the most essential were given full priority. After an initial brainstorming session, we selected solutions to some of the elements of the mechanical design and settled on using stepper motors for the primary drive. The inclusion of high-resolution encoders was planned to allow closed-loop control, but did not make it into the final design as they could not be reliably procured within the build window.

On the programing side of things, we identified the need for a joint solution solver, controller firmware, and some sort of communication interface between the two. Because the mechnical deisgn was to be driven by the electrical systems which were in turn controlled by a microcontroller responding to commands sent by the operator based on the output of the solution engine, it was decided that we would work from opposite ends: one starting with dimensioned designs for the mechanical hardware and the other starting with the joit solution engine.

Kinematics Engine

Complex robotic systems are often described as chains of interconnected links driven at joint interfaces using a Denavit-Hartenburg frame. From this model, the combined position and direction vector for the end effector can be determined based on the transformations induced by different joint angle sets, a method called forward kinematics. Conversely, the joint angle set(s) required to locate and orient the end effector can be computed with greater difficulty using a method called inverse kinematics.
